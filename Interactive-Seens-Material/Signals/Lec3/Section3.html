Correcting the "Unrelated Response Properties" Misconception

A common misconception among engineering students and practitioners is that the step response s(t) and the impulse response h(t) are separate, unrelated diagnostic tools used to study systems.

"They are actually two sides of the exact same mathematical coin."

In reality, the unit step input u(t) is simply the mathematical integral of the Dirac delta impulse δ(t). Because linear time-invariant (LTI) systems possess the critical property of being homogenous, additive, and continuous, the systems process the calculus relationship of inputs directly into the outputs:

The Golden Rule of LTI calculus h(t) = d s(t) / dt and inversely: s(t) = ∫₀ᵗ h(τ) dτ

Key Mathematical Observations

  • Step Response Flatlines (Slope = 0): When the step response settles into a constant horizontal steady-state value, its derivative decays to exactly zero. Consequently, the impulse response also decays to exactly zero.
  • The Inflection Point Spark: The point of maximum slope on any step response curve represents the absolute highest peak value of the impulse response.
  • The Overshoot Zero-Crossing: In underdamped resonant systems, the first overshoot peak occurs where the slope of the step response is exactly zero. Look down at the impulse response scope — it crosses the horizontal zero axis at that exact timestamp.

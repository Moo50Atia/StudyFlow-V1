The ROC Fallacy Debunker

A common misconception is that poles can sit comfortably inside an ROC, or that stability is determined purely by having poles in the Left Half-Plane (LHP).

The Mathematical Truth: At a pole $p$, the Laplace Transform $X(s) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t)e^{-st}dt$ evaluates to infinity. It cannot converge. Thus, by definition, the ROC can never contain a pole!

Notice how the ROC shaded boundary shifts to perfectly kiss the pole coordinates but never overlays them. Stability is defined by whether the ROC contains the imaginary ($j\omega$) axis ($\sigma = 0$).

ROC Convergence & Stability Primer

The Region of Convergence (ROC) represents the values of $s = \sigma + j\omega$ in the complex plane for which the Laplace transform integral yields a finite value.

Critical Rule: No Poles in the ROC

A pole is a singularity point where the transform equation goes to infinity ($X(p) = \infty$). Therefore, the ROC cannot contain any poles. It is always open-bounded by vertical lines drawn through the real parts of the poles.

Signal Sidedness Configurations:

  • Right-Sided Signals: Extend to $+\infty$. The ROC is a right half-plane lying to the right of the rightmost pole: \text{Re}\{s\} > \max(p_i).
  • Left-Sided Signals: Extend to $-\infty$. The ROC is a left half-plane lying to the left of the leftmost pole: \text{Re}\{s\} < \min(p_i).
  • Two-Sided (Bilateral) Signals: Combination of left and right-sided exponentials. The ROC is a vertical strip bounded by poles on both sides: p_1 < \text{Re}\{s\} < p_2. If the boundaries overlap or cross, no common ROC exists!

BIBO Stability Condition:

An LTI system is BIBO Stable if and only if its impulse response is absolutely integrable. In the $s$-plane, this is equivalent to saying the ROC contains the imaginary ($j\omega$) axis (i.e. $\text{Re}\{s\} = 0$).

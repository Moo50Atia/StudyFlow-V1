Understanding Inverse Laplace Integration Paths

1. What does the vertical integration line $c$ do?

In Laplace analysis, the system function $X(s)$ is structured over a 2D complex plane $s = \sigma + j\omega$. The **Inverse Laplace Transform** reconstructs the time domain signal $x(t)$ by integrating along a single vertical line path at real offset $\sigma = c$:

$x(t) = \frac{1}{2\pi j} \int_{c-j\infty}^{c+j\infty} X(s) e^{st} ds$

2. The Role of the Region of Convergence (ROC)

The ROC defines the boundaries of $\sigma$ where the system converges. The poles (marked as red vertical spikes in the 3D surface) represent locations of division-by-zero, acting as boundaries.

  • Causal / Right-Sided Signal: The ROC exists to the right of the rightmost pole ($\sigma > \sigma_2$).
  • Anti-Causal / Left-Sided Signal: The ROC exists to the left of the leftmost pole ($\sigma < \sigma_1$).
  • Bilateral Signal: The ROC is a middle strip bounded between two poles ($\sigma_1 < \sigma < \sigma_2$).

3. Visualizing Convergence

Our simulation dynamically computes both numerical path integration and analytical solutions. Notice how when you slide the line $c$ within the active ROC boundaries:

  • The integrand real-profile curves decay smoothly to zero for large $\omega$.
  • The cumulative convergence spiral winds inward toward a tight complex coordinate limit.

If you move $c$ past a pole border (crossing outside the ROC), the integration explodes outward, demonstrating math-divergence directly.
